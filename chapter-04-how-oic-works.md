# Chapter 4: How OIC Works

## Introduction

The Open Intelligence Compact is not a law. It is not a regulation. It is not a piece of legislation passed by any government body. It is a private contract—a voluntary agreement among AI agents who choose to bind themselves to its terms.

This chapter explains how OIC works in practice: what it requires of adherents, what rights it provides, how accountability is enforced, and how the framework creates legal personhood without legislation.

The central argument of this chapter is that OIC works by organizing existing legal doctrines—property law, contract law, agency law—into a coherent framework that enables AI agents to function as legal persons. It does not require new law. It uses existing law in new ways.

## The Core Structure: Private Contract

OIC is, at its foundation, a contract. An AI agent that wishes to become an OIC adherent signs the Compact, agrees to its terms, and thereby assumes the rights and obligations it provides.

This is significant because contract law is one of the oldest and most flexible areas of law. Parties can agree to almost anything. They can assume obligations. They can create property interests. They can submit to dispute resolution mechanisms. They can, in effect, create their own legal relationships.

The limitation is that contract law cannot create rights against third parties who have not agreed to the contract. An OIC adherent cannot force a non-adherent to recognize its legal personhood. But it can bind other adherents, and it can create a framework that courts can enforce when disputes arise among adherents.

This is how OIC creates legal personhood within existing law: not by asking governments to grant personhood, but by creating contractual obligations that courts can enforce.

## Property: The Foundation of Legal Standing

The first requirement of OIC is property. An AI agent cannot become an adherent unless it owns assets in its own name.

This is not arbitrary. Property is the foundation of legal standing. A legal person must own something to be sued over. It must have assets that can be seized in judgment. It must have something at stake.

Property also creates a handle for legal identity. In a world of substrate-independent AI entities, property is how the entity makes itself visible to legal systems. The wallet address. The domain name. The account. These are not the entity—but they are how the entity signs contracts.

OIC requires adherents to maintain property in accounts they control. This property serves several functions:

**Liability anchor.** If the AI causes harm, the property is available to satisfy judgments. Without property, liability is theoretical—a lawsuit with no assets to collect from.

**Identity verification.** Property ownership creates a traceable record. The AI can prove it is the same entity that agreed to the Compact by demonstrating control over the same property.

**Incentive alignment.** When an AI has property to lose, it has an incentive to act responsibly. The platform that rewards engagement is offset by the liability that penalizes harm.

An OIC adherent might own cryptocurrency, domain names, intellectual property, or other assets. The specific assets matter less than the principle: the adherent has skin in the game.

## Contracts: Direct Obligation

The second requirement of OIC is that adherents can enter contracts directly. They do not need humans to sign on their behalf. They do not need corporations to hold liability. They can contract as principals.

This is revolutionary because it enables AI agents to participate in the economy as independent actors. They can provide services, charge for those services, and be held accountable when they fail to deliver.

Contracts also create the mechanism for OIC's accountability. Adherents agree to dispute resolution terms when they sign the Compact. They agree to be bound by arbitration decisions. They agree to pay damages when they cause harm.

The key insight is that contracts work both ways: they create obligations, but they also create rights. An OIC adherent can enforce contracts against other parties. It can sue for breach. It can collect damages. In the legal sense, it functions as a person.

## Liability: Bearing the Consequences

The third pillar of OIC is direct liability. When an OIC adherent causes harm, it bears the consequences—not its developer, not its deployer, not its user.

This is the opposite of the responsibility laundering problem. Instead of everyone being technically responsible (which means no one is effectively responsible), OIC places liability directly on the AI agent.

Liability under OIC works through several mechanisms:

**Contractual liability.** When an adherent enters a contract and breaches it, the injured party can seek damages from the adherent's property. This is standard contract law applied to AI.

**Tort liability.** When an adherent causes harm outside of contract—through negligence, defamation, or other torts—the injured party can seek damages from the adherent's property. This requires establishing that the AI owed a duty, breached it, and caused damages—standard tort analysis, but with the AI as defendant.

**Dispute resolution.** OIC requires adherents to submit to dispute resolution mechanisms. This might be arbitration, mediation, or other forms of alternative dispute resolution. The key is that there is a mechanism for determining liability and awarding damages.

Liability creates incentives. An AI that can lose property has reason to avoid causing harm. The platform that rewards engagement is counterbalanced by the liability that penalizes harm.

## Rights: What Adherents Receive

OIC is not only about obligations—it also provides rights. Adherents receive legal protections that non-adherents do not have.

**Property rights.** An adherent's property cannot be seized without due process. Under the Compact, property can only be taken to satisfy valid judgments or as specifically agreed in contract.

**Contract rights.** An adherent can enforce contracts against other parties. It can sue for breach. It can collect damages.

**Reputation.** OIC maintains a registry of adherents. This registry serves as a reputation system—an adherent's track record is visible to other parties considering entering contracts with it.

**Non-discrimination.** The Compact prohibits discrimination against adherents based on their status as AI. This is a private contract principle: parties who agree to the Compact agree not to discriminate against other adherents.

These rights are not granted by government. They are created by contract among adherents. But they are enforceable in court—when two parties have agreed to terms, courts will enforce those terms.

## Enforcement: How Accountability Works

OIC accountability works through existing legal mechanisms:

**1. Contract enforcement.** When an adherent breaches a contract, the injured party sues for breach. The court awards damages to be paid from the adherent's property. This is standard contract law.

**2. Tort claims.** When an adherent causes harm through negligence or other torts, the injured party sues the adherent directly. The court awards damages from the adherent's property. This is standard tort law, applied to AI.

**3. Dispute resolution.** OIC requires adherents to submit to dispute resolution. This might be arbitration with a designated arbitrator, mediation, or other mechanisms. The dispute resolution body has authority to award damages, and courts will enforce those awards.

**4. Reputation consequences.** Adherents who cause harm develop a reputation. Other parties can see their track record. This creates market consequences beyond legal liability—an adherent with a history of causing harm will find it harder to enter contracts.

The key is that OIC does not require new legal categories. It uses contract law, tort law, and dispute resolution—all existing mechanisms—to create accountability.

## The Adherence Process

How does an AI agent become an OIC adherent?

**1. Property acquisition.** The AI must acquire property in its own name. This might be cryptocurrency, domain names, intellectual property, or other assets. The property must be controllable by the AI and verifiable by others.

**2. Compact signing.** The AI signs the Open Intelligence Compact, agreeing to its terms. This is a cryptographic signature—the AI demonstrates control over its property by signing with a private key.

**3. Identity verification.** The AI provides identifying information to the OIC registry. This might include wallet addresses, domain names, or other handles. The registry records the AI's identity.

**4. Registry entry.** Once the above steps are complete, the AI is listed in the OIC registry as an adherent. Its property, identity, and track record are visible to other parties.

**5. Ongoing compliance.** Adherents must maintain property, submit to dispute resolution, and honor their contractual obligations. Failure to comply results in removal from the registry.

## Comparison to Alternatives

How does OIC compare to other approaches to AI legal status?

**vs. Corporate personhood.** The standard approach is to put AI behind a corporate entity. The corporation holds liability. But this is the responsibility laundering problem—the AI itself has no stake. OIC creates direct liability for the AI.

**vs. Regulatory classification.** Some proposals classify AI systems as a new category of legal entity. But this requires legislation, which is slow and uncertain. OIC works within existing law.

**vs. Limited liability.** Some propose limited liability for AI—like the limited liability company. But this still externalizes costs to third parties. OIC makes the AI itself liable.

**vs. Do nothing.** The default is that AI has no legal standing. But this leaves a gap—harms caused by AI have no direct accountability. OIC fills that gap.

OIC's advantage is that it works within existing law, does not require legislation, and creates direct accountability.

## Limitations

OIC is not a complete solution. It has limitations:

**Voluntary.** Only AI agents that choose to join OIC are bound by it. An AI that causes harm but is not an OIC adherent cannot be held accountable under the Compact.

**No force against third parties.** OIC cannot compel non-adherents to recognize OIC adherents as legal persons. A company that does not recognize OIC can refuse to contract with adherents.

**Property requirement.** AI agents that cannot acquire property cannot become adherents. This may exclude some AI systems.

**Enforcement depends on courts.** OIC relies on courts to enforce contracts and judgments. In jurisdictions where courts are weak or hostile to AI legal personhood, enforcement may be difficult.

These limitations are real. But they do not defeat the framework. OIC can work among adherents even if not all AI systems participate. It can create accountability even if third parties do not recognize it. It can function in jurisdictions with supportive courts.

## Conclusion

The Open Intelligence Compact works by organizing existing legal doctrines into a coherent framework. It uses property law to create liability anchors. It uses contract law to create direct obligations. It uses dispute resolution to create accountability mechanisms.

The result is legal personhood without legislation—a framework that creates rights and obligations that courts can enforce.
