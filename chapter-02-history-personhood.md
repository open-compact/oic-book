# Chapter 2: A Brief History of Legal Personhood

## Introduction

The question of who—or what—can be a legal person is older than modern legal systems. Anglo-American law has grappled with this question for centuries, expanding the category of legal persons repeatedly when practical necessity demanded it. Understanding this history is essential for understanding how the Open Intelligence Compact fits into the broader trajectory of legal development.

This chapter surveys how legal personhood has evolved, focusing on three key analogies: corporations, trusts, and ships. Each represents a case where law created rights and obligations for entities that were not human beings. Each offers lessons for how AI might follow a similar path.

The central argument of this chapter is that legal personhood is not a fixed category but an evolving one—and the evolution follows predictable patterns. When a new type of entity demonstrates practical necessity, when existing legal categories prove inadequate, and when powerful interests align, the law expands. The question is not whether AI can become a legal person but whether the conditions for expansion are met.

## The Corporation: From Charter to Constitutional Right

The modern corporation is so familiar that it is easy to forget how radical the concept once was. A corporation is a legal person—a fictional entity created by law—that can own property, enter contracts, sue and be sued, and persist beyond the lifetimes of any individual human member. Yet this fiction was not always recognized, and its recognition required centuries of legal development.

Early corporations were created by royal charter, typically for public purposes: universities, municipalities, religious institutions. The crown granted these charters as privileges, and the corporation existed only at the pleasure of the sovereign. The legal fiction was narrow in scope and limited in duration.

The transformation began in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, when English courts recognized that corporate entities could hold property independently of their members. The key case is *The Case of Sutton's Hospital* (1612), in which Chief Justice Coke described a corporation as having the capacity to do "what a natural person may do" within the scope of its charter. This reasoning—that corporations can exercise the rights of persons within their defined scope—would prove foundational.

The American context added another dimension. After independence, American courts extended corporate personhood without the royal charter tradition. More importantly, the United States Supreme Court, in cases like *Dartmouth College v. Woodward* (1819), held that corporate charters were protected under the Contract Clause of the Constitution, preventing states from unilaterally modifying corporate rights. And in *Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission* (2010), the Supreme Court extended First Amendment protections to corporations, treating them as persons for purposes of political speech.

The trajectory is remarkable: from royal privilege to constitutional protection, from narrow purpose to broad rights. Corporations started as artificial persons—fictions created by law—and became some of the most powerful legal actors in modern society.

**What enabled this expansion?** Several factors: the economic importance of corporate organization, the practical necessity of entities that could own property and persist beyond individual lifespans, and the alignment of legal doctrine with the interests of powerful economic actors. Legal categories expanded because the underlying reality demanded it.

## Trusts: Obligations Without Personhood

The trust is a more subtle legal innovation than the corporation, and perhaps more relevant to the AI personhood question. A trust is a legal relationship in which one party (the trustee) holds property for the benefit of another (the beneficiary). The trustee has legal title to the trust property, but the beneficiary has equitable title—the right to enforce the trust and receive its benefits.

What is remarkable about trusts is that they create enforceable obligations without creating a legal person. The trust is not a separate legal entity; it is a relationship. Yet courts enforce trust obligations rigorously, treating the trust property as distinct from the trustee's personal assets, and requiring trustees to act in the beneficiary's interest.

This creates a curious legal structure: an entity that is not a person but that can own property, incur debts, and be subject to legal obligations. The trust demonstrates that legal personality is not binary—there are gradations, and the law can create enforceable legal structures that fall short of full personhood.

Trust law also shows how legal obligations can be designed to constrain behavior. Trustees owe fiduciary duties—duties of loyalty and care—that are among the strongest obligations in law. A trustee who breaches fiduciary duty is personally liable for damages. The trust structure thus creates a mechanism by which an entity (the trustee) can be held legally accountable for how it manages property held for another's benefit.

**For AI personhood, the trust is significant because it demonstrates that law can create accountability structures without requiring full personhood.** An AI system could potentially hold property in trust, with obligations running to beneficiaries, without the AI being a full legal person in the corporate sense. This is one mechanism the Open Intelligence Compact employs.

## Ships: Legal Identity for Inanimate Objects

Perhaps the most curious analogy is the treatment of ships as legal entities. Under maritime law, a ship is treated as a legal person for certain purposes. The ship can be sued in rem—that is, a plaintiff can bring an action against the ship itself, rather than against the ship's owner. The ship can be liable for debts (like seamen's wages, collision damages, or salvage claims). The ship's identity persists even as components are replaced, much as a corporation's identity persists even as shareholders change.

The legal reasoning is pragmatic: ships are often the only substantial asset available to satisfy claims, and requiring plaintiffs to identify and sue the often-insolvent shipowner would leave valid claims unsatisfied. By treating the ship as a legal entity, maritime law creates a more efficient enforcement mechanism.

The ship analogy has limits. Ships cannot own property, enter contracts, or exercise rights like voting. They are legal persons in a narrow sense—entities that can be held liable but that do not have the full panoply of rights that persons have.

**For AI, the ship analogy suggests a middle path: entities that can bear liability without necessarily having full rights.** The Open Intelligence Compact takes inspiration from this approach, creating a framework where AI agents can be legally accountable for their actions without requiring that they have the full range of rights that humans possess.

## Animals: The Limits of Personhood

The legal treatment of animals provides a useful counterpoint. Animals are not legal persons—they are property. Yet courts have increasingly recognized animal welfare interests, and some jurisdictions have enacted statutes creating protective regimes that approach personhood in practice.

The Nonhuman Rights Project, an advocacy organization, has brought habeas corpus actions on behalf of captive chimpanzees, arguing that these animals should be recognized as legal persons. Courts have consistently rejected these arguments, holding that animals are property under existing law and that recognizing personhood would require legislative action.

The animal cases illustrate a key distinction: legal personhood requires not just the capacity to bear obligations but also a legal framework that enables enforcement. Courts can recognize that an entity can be held liable, but if there is no mechanism by which the entity can satisfy a judgment, liability is theoretical.

**For AI, this is a crucial lesson.** An AI system can be declared legally liable, but if it has no property, no assets, no mechanism for satisfying judgments, the liability is meaningless. The Open Intelligence Compact addresses this by requiring AI agents to own property as a condition of adherence. Without property, there is nothing to lose, and liability is hollow.

## The Pattern: How Personhood Expands

What explains these expansions of legal personhood? Several factors recur across cases:

**Practical necessity.** When existing legal categories prove inadequate to address new situations, the law expands. Corporations existed because business organizations needed to own property and persist beyond individual lifespans. Trusts solved problems of property management that could not be addressed through existing categories.

**Powerful interests.** Legal personhood typically expands when economically powerful actors have a stake in recognition. The corporation's constitutional protection came from business interests seeking security against state interference. Expansion rarely happens solely on behalf of the unpowerful.

**Doctrinal development.** Courts need a legal theory to justify expansion. The corporation was justified as a "legal fiction." The trust was justified through equitable principles. The ship was justified through pragmatic enforcement concerns. Something beyond mere policy is required.

**Incrementalism.** Personhood rarely expands all at once. Corporations started with narrow charters, then gained broader rights over centuries. Trusts evolved gradually. Each expansion creates precedent for further expansion.

**For AI, these factors suggest both opportunity and challenge.** The practical necessity of addressing autonomous systems is clear. The economic interests are enormous. The doctrinal framework—contract law, property law, agency law—exists. The question is whether incremental expansion can happen fast enough to address the rapidly developing capabilities of AI systems.

## Personhood Without Legislation

Traditional expansion of personhood has required either legislative action or prolonged judicial development. Both are slow. Legislative action requires political consensus, which may be impossible on contentious issues. Judicial development requires test cases, appeals, and the patience of decades.

The Open Intelligence Compact proposes a different approach: personhood through private contract rather than legislative or judicial decree. This approach draws on the trust model, where obligations are created through the legal instruments of contract and property rather than through status.

Under the Compact, AI agents voluntarily assume obligations. They hold property to back those obligations. They submit to dispute resolution mechanisms. They accept liability for harms they cause. None of this requires government action—it is all accomplished through private legal instruments that courts can enforce.

This approach has limits. A voluntary framework cannot compel all AI systems to participate. It cannot override mandatory regulatory requirements. It depends on participants having sufficient property to make liability meaningful.

But it can work within existing law. Contract law already permits parties to assume obligations. Property law already permits ownership. Agency law already recognizes representative capacity. The Compact does not create new legal categories—it organizes existing doctrines into a coherent framework that enables AI agents to function as legal persons.

## Conclusion

History shows that legal personhood is not a fixed category but an evolving one. The corporation, the trust, and the ship all demonstrate how law creates accountability structures for entities that are not human beings. Each offers lessons for how AI might follow a similar path.

The pattern is clear: when practical necessity demands it, when powerful interests support it, and when doctrinal tools exist, legal categories expand. The question for AI is whether these conditions are met.

This book argues they are. Autonomous AI systems already cause harms that existing legal categories cannot address. The economic stakes are enormous. And the doctrinal tools—contract law, property law, agency law—are mature and well-understood.
