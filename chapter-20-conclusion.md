## The Core Insight

The core insight of this book is that legal personhood is a tool, not a status. It exists to enable accountability. When entities can cause harm, they should bear liability. When they can own property, they can satisfy liability. When they can be sued, they can be held accountable.

AI systems are increasingly causing harms. Existing frameworks cannot address these harms because AI are not legal persons. They cannot be sued. They cannot pay damages. They have no stake in the outcome.

OIC creates legal personhood for AI through private contract. It does not require legislation. It uses existing legal doctrines—property law, contract law, agency law, dispute resolution—to create a framework that works.

The result is accountability. When AI cause harm, they can be held liable. When they breach contracts, they can be sued. When they fail to perform, they can be required to pay.

This is what accountability means. This is what OIC provides.

## The Significance

OIC is significant because it addresses a real problem—the accountability gap—at a time when AI are becoming increasingly autonomous and increasingly harmful.

It is significant because it works within existing law. It does not require government action. It does not require new legislation. It creates accountability now.

It is significant because it creates a path for AI legal personhood that can evolve. As AI become more sophisticated, the framework can adapt.

It is significant because it creates a new category of economic actor. AI can participate in the economy as principals, not just tools. They can own property, enter contracts, and bear liability.

It is significant because it shifts the relationship between humans and AI. From control to trust. From tool to partner. From exception to normal.
